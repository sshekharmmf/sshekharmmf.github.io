About Project


Summary:Latitude Vs X


The purpose of this project was to analyze how wheather changes
as you get closer to the equator.In order to achieve This,
first we pulled data from the OpenWeatherMap API to assemble a dataset of 500 cities
After assembling the dataset , we used Matplotib to plot various
aspects of the weather vrs latitude.Factors we looked at included
Temperature,Cloudiness,windspeed and Humidity.This site provides the source data
and the visualizations created as part of the analysis, as well as the explanations and describtions of any
trends and correlations withnessed.

Cloudiness


Continents tend to be less cloudy than adjacent oceans
(at the same latitude),
except in the equatorial belt. On average,
cloudiness is highest around 60◦S
(about 80% of the time) but there is
only 40% cloud cover at the South Pole.
These figures reflect the ascent and subsidence
occurring at these respective latitudes,
and the large amount of ocean at around 60◦S.
Satellite-based cloudiness estimates
over surfaces covered by snow or ice are
somewhat uncertain, especially at night.

WindSpeed


Air pressure and winds depends indirectly on latitude
of the place.If a place is away from the equator,
it receives less sunlight and a rise in latitude
leads to a reduction in air pressure and
a drop in latitude means that air pressure increases because
of the weight of the air column.

Max Temperature


As expected, the weather becomes significantly warmer
as one approaches the equator(0 Degrees Latitude).
More insterestingly, however , is the fact that
the southern hemispher
tends to be warmer this time of the year than the
northern hemisphere.This may be due to the
tilt of the earth at the time of the year the
data was gathered.

Humidity


Humidity is at a maximum in the Tropics and declines as one
moves poleward reaching minimum values in each polar
region.This implies that the air in the most "arid"
desert regions of the planet
(30 degrees N or S latitude) actually contains
more water vapor than temperate regions much
farther north, and certainly more than
the polar regions.
